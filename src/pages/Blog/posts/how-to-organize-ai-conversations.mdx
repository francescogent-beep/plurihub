# How to Organise Your AI Conversations (Before You Lose Another Good One)

There is a conversation you had with an AI tool a few weeks ago. You remember it was useful. You remember roughly what it was about. You cannot find it.

You've tried scrolling through ChatGPT's sidebar. You've tried searching. You probably gave up and started a new conversation, re-explaining the context from scratch.

This is the most common problem AI power users face, and it gets worse the more you use these tools. Here is how to fix it.

## Why AI history fails you

Every major AI platform — ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Perplexity — organises your conversation history the same way: by recency. Today's conversations are at the top. Last month's are buried somewhere below 200 other entries.

There is no folder system. No tags. No way to group conversations by project or topic. If you use AI seriously, this becomes a problem within the first month.

The situation is even worse if you use multiple AI tools, because your conversations are split across completely separate platforms. Your ChatGPT history is in ChatGPT. Your Claude history is in Claude. You have to remember not just what you discussed, but which tool you used to discuss it.

## The system that works

The simplest organisation system for AI conversations has three layers:

**1. Save selectively.** Not every conversation is worth keeping. Save the ones where you got something genuinely useful: a framework, a draft, a plan, a piece of research. Ignore the throwaway queries.

**2. Organise by project, not by tool.** The natural instinct is to think "my ChatGPT stuff is here, my Claude stuff is there." That's not how your brain retrieves information. Your brain retrieves by project — "that client proposal," "that market research," "my book outline." Your AI conversations should be organised the same way.

**3. Keep your best prompts.** The prompts that produce your best results are worth saving and reusing. If you found a way to ask Claude for feedback that actually gives you useful feedback, save that prompt. Use it again next time.

## What PluriHub doesn't do

It's worth being clear about this: PluriHub does not read your conversations. It saves the link to the conversation, not the text. Your actual chat content stays exactly where it always was — in ChatGPT's servers, in Claude's servers, wherever you had the conversation.

This matters for privacy. PluriHub is purely a navigation and organisation layer. It gives your browser's sidebar a memory for where your important conversations are. Nothing more.
